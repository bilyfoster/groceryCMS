### Post 1: Navigating Insurance for Mental Health Care: A Comprehensive Guide
**Author:** Clarke Scott, LPC  
**Date:** May 15  
**Read Time:** 5 min

#### Key Terms & Definitions
- **Premium** — The monthly amount paid to keep insurance active, regardless of usage.
- **Deductible** — The out-of-pocket amount paid before insurance shares costs (therapy sessions count toward this).
- **Copay** — A flat fee per session (e.g., $30) paid after meeting the deductible. Common with HMO plans.
- **Coinsurance** — A percentage of session cost shared with insurance (e.g., 20% patient / 80% insurer) after deductible is met.
- **Out-of-Pocket Max** — The maximum paid in a plan year; insurance covers 100% after this limit is reached.
- **In-Network** — Therapists with a contract to the insurer (usually cheaper).
- **Out-of-Network** — Therapists without a contract; partial reimbursement may apply.
- **Superbill** — An itemized receipt from your therapist for out-of-network reimbursement submission.
- **Authorization** — Pre-approval required by some plans before therapy begins (often handled by therapist's office).

#### Step-by-Step Insurance Process
1. **Check Your Benefits:** Call member services or log into the insurer's portal to ask about outpatient mental health benefits, deductibles, and referral requirements.
2. **Find an In-Network Therapist:** Use the insurer's provider directory filtered by "mental health." **Call directly** to confirm they are accepting new clients and remain in-network (directories are often outdated).
3. **Confirm Coverage:** Ask the therapist's office to verify benefits before starting (session cost, deductible application, pre-authorization needs).
4. **Attend Session:** Therapist submits claim using a diagnosis code (standard practice for payment).
5. **Pay & Track:** Receive an Explanation of Benefits (EOB) to track deductible/out-of-pocket max progress.

> **Heads Up:** Insurance directories are often outdated. Always call a therapist directly to confirm they're accepting new clients and are still in-network with your plan before scheduling.

#### Financial Alternatives (If Not In-Network)
- **Out-of-Network Reimbursement:** Submit a superbill to your insurer.
    - *Reimbursement Rate:* Commonly **40–70%** for plans with these benefits (common in PPOs).
- **HSA / FSA Accounts:** Therapy is a qualified medical expense.
    - *Benefit:* Pay tax-free; effectively reduces cost by **20–35%** depending on tax bracket.
- **Sliding Scale & Intern Rates:** Reduced-cost care pathways offered by Brazen Therapy.
    - **Sliding Scale:** Licensed therapists offer reduced rates based on financial situation.
    - **Intern Rates:** Graduate-level clinicians (supervised) offer significantly lower rates for tight budgets.

#### Common Questions & Answers
- **Employer Plans:** Most are required to include mental health coverage under the Mental Health Parity Act. Verify session limits and copays via HR or benefits portal.
- **Session Limits:** Most modern plans cover "medically necessary" treatment without strict limits, though some have annual caps. Verify with member services.
- **Privacy:** Insurer may report aggregate utilization to employer, but individual details are protected under HIPAA. Paying out-of-pocket keeps records entirely private.
- **Copay vs. Coinsurance:** Copays are flat fees (predictable); coinsurance is a percentage of the allowed amount (variable).
- **Online Therapy:** Most major insurers cover video sessions at rates equal to in-person visits.
- **Claim Denials:** You have the right to appeal. Common reasons include administrative errors or missing pre-authorization; appeals are often successful with proper documentation.

> **Figuring out insurance shouldn't stand between you and the support you deserve.** Our team is happy to verify your benefits and answer any questions before your first session.

### Post 2: What is Autistic Burnout? A Guide to Recovery and Prevention
**Author:** Erica Marisella Harris, LPC  
**Date:** Apr 8  
**Read Time:** 4 min

#### Overview
Autistic burnout is a distinct physiological event, differing significantly from neurotypical "professional burnout." While not fully detailed in the **DSM-5**, it is a common reality for neurodiverse individuals, often described by clients as a "total system shutdown" rather than simple tiredness.

> *"Autistic burnout exhaustion is a total system shutdown. It's a physical and mental plea that says: 'I need absolute quiet. The lights must be dimmed. I need my softest clothes without tags or irritating seams...'"*

> *"Burnout is not a character flaw. It is a physiological event, no different than catching a cold."*

> *"Sensory input is what your body feels (like a tight hug), while sensory output is the physical response that leads to feeling mentally calmer after the input."*

#### Symptoms & Signs
Autistic burnout manifests through specific physiological and cognitive changes:

- **Chronic Exhaustion:** A deep, systemic fatigue requiring rest beyond typical relaxation (e.g., Netflix/charcuterie).
- **Loss of Skills:** Impacts cognition, executive functioning (prioritizing/planning), memory, and speech. Some may become temporarily non-speaking or lose ability to handle daily tasks.
- **Increased Sensory Sensitivity:** "Sensory overload" where clothing, sounds (e.g., fan hum), or textures feel like physical assaults. Leads to frequent meltdowns or shutdowns.
- **Increased Stimming:** Heightened need for vocalizing, hand movements, or muscle tensing to self-soothe and regulate.

#### Primary Causes
- **Masking:** The emotional/physical toll of repressing natural behaviors (like stimming) to "pass" as non-autistic.
- **Major Life Transitions:** New jobs, relocation, school changes, or new pets/cohabitants.
- **Cumulative Stress:** Multiple small changes happening simultaneously, causing difficulty to adjust.
- **External Expectations:** Pressure to succeed by neurotypical standards, leading to a sense of "perpetual failure."

#### Path to Recovery & Prevention

##### 1. Protect Your Energy (Red, Yellow, Green System)
Focus on **conserving** energy rather than optimizing it. Use a traffic light analogy instead of Spoon Theory:
- **🔴 Red Days (Stop):** Basic survival and rest. Example: Waking up with the flu; crawl back into bed rather than organizing cabinets.
- **🟡 Yellow Days (Go Slow):** Have some strength. Take care of basics without overdoing it.
- **🟢 Green Days (Go):** Tackle bigger "optional" tasks when energy permits.

**Energy Protection Tips:**
- **The "Bare Minimum + One" Rule:** Meet basic needs (hydration, food, hygiene), then choose exactly **one** optional task.
- **Scheduled Rest:** Build in "rest without productivity" (e.g., sit in your car for 10 minutes after work before going inside).
- **Track Energy:** Notice patterns of what drains vs. fuels you, not just mood.

##### 2. Validation & Radical Self-Talk
- **Find Your "Circle":** Connect with a therapist or friend who understands and won't judge your Red Days.
- **The "Toothbrush" Win:** Acknowledge small accomplishments (like brushing teeth) on Red Days.
- **Separate Identity from Output:** Reflect on who you are outside of productivity (values, special interests).

##### 3. Build a Daily Sensory Diet
Maintain preventative maintenance for your nervous system even when feeling "fine." Use tools to identify inputs (weighted blankets, fidgets) and outputs (heavy work, wall pushes) that regulate your system.

### Post 3: Therapy Isn't Just for Crisis: Why People Start Therapy Even When Life Feels Fine
**Author:** Kira McSherry, Master of Counseling Intern  
**Date:** Mar 23  
**Read Time:** 3 min

#### Key Excerpts & Quotes
> "Therapy isn't a competition about who is struggling the most. You don't need a diagnosis or a crisis to deserve support."

> "Life might look okay from the outside, work is fine, relationships are stable enough, but internally, something feels off."

> "Therapy helps shift the focus from 'What's wrong with me?' to 'Where did this pattern come from, and is it still helping me?'"

> "Sometimes the reason people start therapy is simple: they want things to feel easier or more aligned. That's enough."

> "Therapy can be incredibly supportive during hard seasons, but it can also be a place for growth, clarity, and self-understanding long before things feel overwhelming."

---

#### Core Concepts & Insights

##### 1. The Myth of Crisis-Only Therapy
Many assume therapy is reserved for major life crises (e.g., relationship endings, overwhelming anxiety, grief). While true, this is only part of the story. Common barriers include:
- "I don't think my problems are big enough."
- "Other people have it worse."
- "Nothing bad happened, so I don't know if I need therapy."

##### 2. Therapy as Preventative Care
Mental health requires maintenance similar to physical health (checkups, healthy habits). Starting therapy early helps:
- Recognize stress before it becomes burnout.
- Notice recurring patterns in relationships.
- Learn coping mechanisms for anxiety or overwhelm.
- Improve communication and emotional awareness.
- Build resilience before life feels heavy.

##### 3. Understanding Patterns vs. Self-Judgment
Therapy shifts the internal narrative from self-blame to curiosity:
- **Old Focus:** "Why do I overthink?" / "Why do I shut down?"
- **New Focus:** "Where did this pattern come from, and is it still helping me?"
- **Outcome:** Understanding patterns grants choice, allowing you to respond with awareness rather than reacting automatically.

##### 4. Therapy for Life Transitions
Transitions are common reasons to start therapy, even if they feel positive. Examples include:
- Starting or ending a relationship.
- Becoming a parent.
- Career shifts, burnout, or moving to a new city.
- Entering a new stage of adulthood.
- Changes within family roles, identity, physical capabilities, or school.
- Receiving a major diagnosis.

> **Note:** Even positive changes can feel destabilizing; excitement and uncertainty often coexist.

---

#### Actionable Indicators: Signs You Might Benefit
You do not need to wait until things feel overwhelming. Therapy may be helpful if you experience:
- Feeling stuck or unsure what's next.
- Noticing repeating patterns in relationships.
- Stress or anxiety feels harder to manage lately.
- Feeling disconnected from yourself or others.
- Wanting better communication or boundaries.
- Life looks "fine," but you don't quite feel like yourself.
- Wanting support while navigating change.

#### Long-Term Benefits & Outcomes
Therapy requires persistence, but small changes accumulate into meaningful change over time:
- Pausing before reacting.
- Communicating more clearly.
- Feeling less overwhelmed by everyday stress.
- Understanding emotions sooner.
- Starting to trust yourself more.

**Conclusion:** If you are wondering if starting therapy makes sense even when life feels mostly okay, that curiosity alone is a meaningful place to begin.
